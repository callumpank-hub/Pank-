01 — About

Callum Pankhurst is a New Zealand–based artist and designer whose practice blends traditional handcraft with a contemporary sensitivity to material and form. With a professional background in interior architecture, product design and manufacturing, his approach is rooted in deep experience, ethical intent, and a respect for process.

His work — whether a commercial interior or a fine art piece — is unified by the same instincts: natural materials, honest construction, and a preference for pieces built to last rather than to impress at a glance. Recent work spans abstract and landscape-driven compositions, built through slow, manual techniques — layering plaster, pigment and texture through carving, erosion and organic transformation.

02 — Design Portfolio

Twenty-plus years across architecture, interiors and brand-integrated spaces.

Projects that favour honest materials and a strong response to site over decoration — black-clad forms set against hillside grass and open sky, small-footprint builds that don't waste a square metre, and one-off houses designed to be lived in for decades, not photographed once and forgotten.

Fit-outs for teams who spend real hours in these rooms — timber-lined studios, street-facing signage that doubles as branding, and layouts built around focus and collaboration rather than showroom polish.

Spaces where the design has to survive a Friday night crowd and still feel considered on a quiet Tuesday — exposed materials, low lighting, and identity work integrated into the build rather than added afterwards.

Retail fit-outs built to hold a brand's identity in three dimensions — shopfronts, display joinery and lighting designed together rather than layered on top of a generic shell.

Interior work for houses already built — kitchens, living spaces and custom joinery that respond to how a household actually moves through a home, with the same material honesty as the architecture work.

The in-between projects — a bar's signage cut into stone and timber, a food trailer built to run a wood-fired grill on the road, a bike shelter for a public space. Proof the same design thinking scales down as well as it scales up.
